## Primary Survey — CABCDE Approach

Every medical emergency begins with CABCDE in the first 5 minutes.
C — Control life-threatening bleeding. Compress the site. If uncontrolled, pack the wound. If still uncontrolled, apply a tourniquet above the injury. Document time of application.
A — Check airway is open. If not: head-tilt chin-lift. Use jaw thrust if trauma suspected.

---

## Primary Survey — Airway and Breathing

If airway is blocked: suction as needed. Use OPA or NPA airway adjunct. Do not use NPA if facial trauma is present. Immobilize cervical spine if trauma is indicated.
Assess breathing: if no or inadequate breathing, give BVM ventilations. Rate: 10–15 per minute for adults, 20–30 per minute for children.

---

## Primary Survey — Oxygen Administration

Mild to moderate difficulty breathing: nasal cannula 2–6 litres per minute.
Severe difficulty breathing: non-rebreather mask 10–15 litres per minute.
If not improving: BVM-assisted ventilations at 15 litres per minute.
Target oxygen saturation above 92% where monitoring is available.

---

## Primary Survey — Circulation and Shock Recognition

Treat as shock if SBP greater than 90 AND any 2 of: HR above 120, capillary refill above 3 seconds, RR below 10 or above 22, decreased GCS, sweating or clammy skin, cold extremities, restless or combative.
OR if SBP below 90 AND any 1 of those signs.

---

## Primary Survey — Disability and Glucose

Assess level of consciousness. If glucose is low or cannot be checked, give glucose immediately.
Buccal route: smear 25g oral glucose on gums or inside cheek.
IV route: give 20 mL of 50% glucose. Recheck after 5 minutes. Repeat if no improvement.
Always exclude hypoglycaemia as a cause of altered mental status.

---

## Primary Survey — Vital Signs and History

Obtain: respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, heart rate, blood pressure, temperature.
Take SAMPLE history: Signs and symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past medical history, Last oral intake, Events leading to emergency.
Always ask women about last menstrual period — consider pregnancy or recent delivery.

---

## Normal Adult Vital Signs

Pulse rate: 60–100 beats per minute.
Respiratory rate: 10–20 breaths per minute.
Systolic blood pressure: above 90 mmHg.
Oxygen saturation: above 92%.
Estimating blood pressure by pulse: carotid pulse indicates SBP at least 60 mmHg; femoral pulse indicates at least 70 mmHg; radial pulse indicates at least 80 mmHg.

---

## Patient in Shock — General Management

Lay patient flat. Exception: suspected cardiogenic shock — sit upright.
Keep patient warm. Apply cardiac monitor if available.
IV fluid resuscitation for non-traumatic shock: adults 250–500 mL bolus over 5 minutes. Children: 20 mL/kg over 10 minutes. Malnourished children: 10 mL/kg over 10 minutes.

---

## Patient in Shock — Cardiogenic Shock

Cardiogenic shock is likely if patient has: pacemaker, swollen ankles, breathlessness worse lying down, raised neck veins, wet cough, cardiac history, crackles on listening to chest.
Management: do NOT give IV fluids. Place IV line. Sit patient upright. Apply cardiac monitor. Do not give fluid bolus.

---

## Patient in Shock — Active Bleeding

Active bleeding is likely if: obvious blood loss, pulsating or growing abdominal swelling, vomiting or coughing blood, rectal or vaginal bleeding.
Give oral rehydration solution if alert and protecting airway. IV fluid: 100–250 mL bolus every 10 minutes. For trauma: give Tranexamic acid within 3 hours of injury.

---

## Patient with Difficulty Breathing

Sit patient up or raise head of stretcher.
Wheezing: give bronchodilator (Salbutamol inhaler).
Severe asthma or anaphylaxis: give Adrenaline IM. Repeat after 20 minutes if no improvement.
Crackles with raised neck veins: sit upright, give oxygen. Consider cardiac cause and follow chest pain protocol.

---

## Patient with Difficulty Breathing — Cardiac Cause

If cardiac condition is suspected: give Aspirin 300 mg chewed. Assist patient to take their own Glyceryl trinitrate. Ensure systolic blood pressure is above 100 mmHg before giving nitrates.
If still struggling to breathe or agitated: support breathing with BVM ventilations.

---

## Patient with Difficulty Breathing — Croup or Epiglottitis

Reassure the patient. Keep them calm. Give humidified oxygen.
In small children: sit on parent's lap. Parent holds oxygen mask near face. Do not force the mask on the child. Avoid agitating the child as this worsens airway obstruction.

---

## Choking Patient — Signs and Assessment

Signs of choking: history of foreign body, abnormal sounds such as stridor or grunting, patient placing hands on neck.
If conscious and coughing or making noise: keep calm, encourage coughing, reassess frequently. Coughing is the most effective way to clear a foreign body.

---

## Choking — Conscious and Not Coughing

Adult or larger child: 5 abdominal thrusts followed by 5 back blows. Repeat until foreign body is removed, person can speak or cough, or they become unconscious.
Pregnant patient: 5 back blows followed by 5 chest thrusts.
Infant or small child: 5 back blows followed by 5 chest thrusts.

---

## Choking — Unconscious Patient

Open airway. Remove foreign body if visible. Begin chest compressions per local protocol.
Repeat cycle until obstruction is relieved. Advanced providers: laryngoscopy with Magill forceps to remove foreign body. Intubate and attempt to ventilate through the obstruction if needed.

---

## Chest Pain or Palpitations

Apply cardiac monitor or perform 12-lead ECG. If no pulse, begin CPR per local protocol.
Likely cardiac condition: give Aspirin 300 mg chewed. Assist patient with their own Glyceryl trinitrate. Ensure systolic BP above 100 mmHg. Advanced: give Glyceryl trinitrate or Isosorbide dinitrate.

---

## Chest Pain — Arrhythmia

Heart rate too fast or too slow: look for and treat the cause.
For tachyarrhythmia: manual manoeuvres such as vagal manoeuvres. Advanced: cardioversion.
For bradyarrhythmia: advanced providers give Atropine or Adrenaline.
Fever: give antipyretic. Wheezing: give bronchodilator.

---

## Altered Mental Status — Assessment

Always exclude hypoglycaemia first as a cause of altered mental status. Check glucose immediately.
Then assess for: shock — use shock protocol. Blood pressure above 180/110 — use stroke protocol. Abnormal pupils. Current seizure. Inability to speak or move one side.

---

## Altered Mental Status — Pupils

Pinpoint pupils with low respiratory rate: consider opioid overdose. Give Naloxone.
Unequal pupils: consider head bleed or hidden trauma.
Dilated pupils: consider poisoning. Follow poisoning protocol.
If no medical or traumatic cause found: consider behavioural emergency. Use soft restraints per local protocol if patient is combative.

---

## Seizures — Active Seizure Management

Create a safe space. Move hard and sharp objects away from the patient. Open airway. Suction if needed. Give oxygen.
If pregnant or recently delivered: give Magnesium sulfate IM and IV. If seizure continues after 15 minutes, give second IV dose.
Non-pregnant: give Diazepam PR or IV, or Midazolam IV.

---

## Seizures — Post-Seizure Care

After seizure ends: evaluate for signs of trauma. If trauma present, follow trauma protocol. Evaluate for suspected stroke. If stroke signs present, follow stroke protocol.
Place patient in recovery position if no trauma. Monitor airway closely throughout transport to hospital.

---

## Injured Patient — Initial Assessment

Immobilize cervical spine if indicated. In head injury, avoid use of collar.
Consider NOT immobilizing if: penetrating trauma with no neurological deficit, OR patient is alert with GCS 15 and no distracting injury, no neck tenderness, no neck pain.

---

## Injured Patient — Chest and Shock

Chest injury management: sucking chest wound — apply 3-sided dressing. Flail chest — control pain, monitor breathing. Tension pneumothorax — needle decompression by advanced providers.
Shock: control bleeding. IV fluids 100–250 mL bolus, repeat to keep SBP above 90. Give Tranexamic acid for trauma bleeding within 3 hours.

---

## Injured Patient — Secondary Survey

Remove all clothing. Fully expose for secondary survey. Cover with blanket and keep warm.
Splint extremity injuries. Bandage wounds. Abdominal injuries with visible bowel: do not replace into body — cover with sterile moist gauze. Amputations: place body part in moist wrap. Impaled object: do not remove — stabilize in place. Control pain.

---

## Burns — Stop the Burning Process

Stop, drop, roll. Cool with room temperature running water. Decontaminate chemical burns immediately.
Monitor airway closely — inhalation burns can rapidly cause swelling. Signs of airway burns: facial burns, soot around mouth or nose, black sputum, singed nasal hairs, hoarse voice. Advanced: consider early intubation.

---

## Burns — Fluid Management by TBSA

TBSA below 20%: oral fluids to thirst.
TBSA 20–40%: if alert and protecting airway, give oral rehydration solution. IV fluids per standard formula.
TBSA above 40%: IV fluids per standard formula.
Formula: 4 mL × weight in kg × percent TBSA. Give half in first 8 hours from time of burn. Give analgesia. Dress burned areas.

---

## Burns — Dressing and Specialist Referral

Dress burns with plastic wrap, clean dry sheet, or blanket. Document or photograph before dressing. Remove clothing and jewelry. Elevate burned limbs.
Burns requiring specialist care: partial or full thickness above 15% TBSA (above 10% in children), burns of hands, face, groin or joints, circumferential burns, inhalation injury, burns with associated trauma, burns in the very young or elderly.

---

## Earthquake-Related Injuries — Crush and Trauma

During or after an earthquake, expect: crush injuries, fractures, head trauma, internal bleeding, and entrapment injuries.
Follow the Injured Patient protocol. Immobilize spine if indicated. Control active bleeding with direct pressure and tourniquet if needed. Give Tranexamic acid within 3 hours for traumatic bleeding.

---

## Earthquake — Crush Syndrome (Rhabdomyolysis)

Patients trapped for more than 1 hour who are then freed may develop crush syndrome. Expect: dark urine, muscle swelling, kidney failure risk.
Start IV fluids early and aggressively before and during extrication if possible. Do not apply tourniquet to a crush injury unless bleeding is life-threatening. Rapid transport to hospital is critical.

---

## Earthquake — Breathing Problems After Entrapment

Dust inhalation and chest compression from rubble can cause breathing difficulty. Treat as breathing difficulty protocol. Give oxygen.
Check for pneumothorax if chest was compressed: unequal chest rise, absent breath sounds on one side, worsening hypoxia. Advanced: needle decompression for tension pneumothorax.

---

## Earthquake — Altered Mental Status After Trauma

Head injury from falling debris is common in earthquakes. Follow altered mental status protocol.
Assess pupils. Unequal pupils suggest head bleed. Raise head of stretcher if head injury is suspected. Avoid collar in head injury. Maintain oxygen saturation above 92%. Transport urgently to closest appropriate facility.

---

## Earthquake — Hypothermia After Entrapment

Patients trapped for prolonged periods may develop hypothermia, especially at night. Remove wet clothing, protect modesty, wrap in blankets and cover head.
Warm vehicle with heat. Handle patient gently. Use warm IV fluids if available. Monitor for afterdrop as patient warms. Kangaroo care for infants.

---

## Earthquake — Psychological and Behavioural Response

Panic, aggression, dissociation, and shock are common after earthquakes. Always consider medical or traumatic cause first before treating as behavioural emergency. Check glucose.
Try to calm the patient. Ensure your own safety. Use soft restraints only if danger to self or others. Obtain information from bystanders about what the patient experienced.

---

## Abdominal Pain

Assess for cardiac cause first — if suspected, follow chest pain protocol. If fever is present, give antipyretic. Give antibiotics if long transport time per local protocol.
Consider pregnancy complications in any woman of childbearing age. Do not give anything to eat or drink. Give analgesia and antiemetic for nausea or vomiting.

---

## Drowning Survivor

Remove patient from water if safe. Begin CPR if no pulse. Handle gently. Monitor airway closely. Keep patient warm.
Check for hypothermia — follow environmental protocol if present. Check for other injuries — follow trauma protocol if present. Drowning survivors should receive prolonged resuscitation efforts — outcomes are possible even after extended submersion.

---

## Heat Emergency

Remove patient from hot environment. Remove clothing and protect modesty, for example cover with wet sheet. Cool with moist cloths and fan. Cool vehicle with air conditioning or open window.
If alert and protecting airway: give cold oral fluids. IV fluids cooled if possible.

---

## Cold Emergency (Hypothermia)

Remove from cold environment. Remove all wet clothing. Prevent further heat loss — wrap in blankets and cover head. Use warmed blankets where possible. Warm vehicle with heat.
Handle patient gently. Active non-invasive rewarming: heat packs, heat lamps, forced air systems. Monitor for afterdrop as patient warms. Use warm IV fluids.

---

## Pain Management

Assess pain level using a pain score. Document score at least twice.
Non-medication approaches: verbal reassurance, ice packs, immobilization.
Mild to moderate pain: give Paracetamol (acetaminophen). Adults: 500 mg–1 g every 6 hours.
Severe pain: advanced providers give IV Morphine. Monitor breathing closely.
Manage nausea or vomiting with antiemetic (Metoclopramide or Ondansetron).

---

## Suspected Sepsis — Adults

Suspect sepsis in adults if all 3 present: evidence of infection AND physiologic derangement AND any of — heart rate above 90, respiratory rate above 20, temperature below 36°C or above 38°C, confusion or altered mental status, signs of shock.
Severe sepsis signs: HR above 130, RR above 30, SBP below 90, VP or U on AVPU.

---

## Suspected Sepsis — Management

IV fluids: adults 30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus. Children: 20 mL/kg. Malnourished children: 10 mL/kg. If shock persists, repeat bolus. If shock persists after second bolus, give IV vasopressors.
Fever: give antipyretic. Give antibiotics if long transport time per local protocol. Identify potential infection source. Wound cleaning if long transport time.

---

## Suspected Sepsis — Children

Suspect sepsis in children if: evidence of infection AND signs of shock — feeble rapid pulse, rapid breathing, cool or pale extremities, altered mental status.
Management same as adults adjusted for weight. Children: 20 mL/kg crystalloid bolus. Malnourished children: 10 mL/kg. Repeat if shock signs persist.

---

## Suspected Stroke — FAST Assessment

FAST: Facial movements — abnormal or asymmetric. Arm drift. Speech impairment. Time — note time of onset.
If stroke is possible: transport immediately to closest stroke-capable facility. Time from symptom onset to treatment is critical. Do not delay transport for assessment.

---

## Suspected Stroke — Additional Assessment

Assess pupils: pinpoint with low respiratory rate — consider opioid overdose, give Naloxone. Unequal — consider head bleed or trauma. Dilated — consider poisoning.
Check for current seizure — follow seizure protocol if present. Check for poisoning — follow poisoning protocol if present.

---

## Poisoning and Overdose — Scene Safety

Do not enter scene if there is concern for ongoing chemical exposure. Decontaminate patient: remove clothes and wash skin with soap and water. Flush eyes with running water or normal saline.
Do NOT induce vomiting. If shock: give IV fluid bolus and repeat as needed. If seizure: give Diazepam PR or IV, or Midazolam IV.

---

## Poisoning — Toxidromes: Opioid and Cholinergic

Opioid: decreased consciousness, pinpoint pupils, low respiratory rate, low heart rate, hypotension. Give Naloxone immediately.
Cholinergic (organophosphate/nerve agent): decreased consciousness, seizures, pinpoint pupils, low heart rate, urine incontinence, diarrhoea and vomiting. Give Atropine.

---

## Poisoning — Toxidromes: Sympathomimetic, Anticholinergic, Sedative

Sympathomimetic (stimulants, cocaine): seizures, sweating, agitation, high temperature, high heart rate, high blood pressure, dilated pupils. Symptomatic treatment.
Anticholinergic: dry flushed skin, agitation, high temperature, high heart rate, dilated pupils, urine retention, blurred vision. Symptomatic treatment.
Sedative: decreased consciousness, low respiratory rate, slurred speech. Symptomatic treatment.

---

## Poisoning — Activated Charcoal

Give activated charcoal if: ingestion was less than 60 minutes ago AND patient is alert and protecting airway AND substance ingested was NOT alcohol, petrol, paraffin, corrosives, household products, iron, or lithium.
Adults: 50–100 g. Children: 1 g per kg. Administer orally.

---

## Syncope

If no pulse: begin CPR. Assess circulation. If issues: give IV fluids and apply cardiac monitor.
If heart rate too fast or too slow: treat arrhythmia per local protocol. Identify potential cause — was there a seizure? Is patient pregnant? Was there chest pain? Is there history of trauma or GI bleeding?

---

## Snakebite

Ensure your own safety. Take a photo of the snake if possible. Do NOT attempt to catch the snake.
DO NOT apply tourniquet around the bite or limb. DO NOT cut the bite. DO NOT suck on the bite.
Restrict patient movement. Splint the limb to immobilize as much as possible. Note time of splinting. Follow local protocol for snakes in your area. Transport urgently.

---

## Visibly Pregnant Woman — General

Always position visibly pregnant patient in the left lateral recumbent position to avoid hypotension during assessment and transport.
If seizures: give Magnesium sulfate IM and IV. If seizure continues after 15 minutes, give second IV dose.

---

## Pregnant Woman — Labour and Delivery

Active labour with urge to push: check for crowning. No crowning: rapid transport. Head visible: prepare for birth. Presenting part is not the head: do not attempt delivery, transport urgently to surgical facility.
Active management of third stage: give Oxytocin IM or Carbetocin IM or Misoprostol within 1 minute of birth.

---

## Postpartum Haemorrhage (PPH)

Use MOTIV bundle within 15 minutes: Massage the uterus, Oxytocic drug, Tranexamic acid 1 g IV as soon as possible, IV access. Give all elements even if there is improvement.
If still bleeding: bimanual compression of uterus or external aortic compression. Consider non-pneumatic anti-shock garment. Reassess and transport immediately.

---

## Neonatal Resuscitation — At Birth

Dry newborn thoroughly. If breathing or crying: keep warm, wrap in blanket and cap, in warm environment.
If not breathing: keep warm, stimulate by rubbing or flicking feet and rubbing back. Position airway in neutral position with a small cloth under shoulders. Reevaluate breathing and check heart rate.

---

## Neonatal Resuscitation — BVM and Compressions

Breathing inadequate or HR below 100 bpm: BVM ventilations at 40 breaths per minute. Observe for chest rise. If breathing above 30 per minute or crying, stop ventilating.
HR below 60 bpm after 1 minute of ventilations: chest compressions. 2 rescuers: 3 compressions then 1 ventilation at 120 events per minute. 1 rescuer: 30 compressions then 2 ventilations. Stop compressions if HR above 60.

---

## Key Medications — Aspirin

Indication: suspected acute coronary syndrome.
Dose: 300 mg orally, preferably chewed or dissolved in water, as a single immediate dose.

---

## Key Medications — Adrenaline (Epinephrine)

Indication: anaphylaxis, severe allergic reaction, severe asthma.
Adult dose 50 kg or above: 0.5 mg IM. 40 kg: 0.4 mg IM. 30 kg: 0.3 mg IM. Repeat every 5 minutes as needed.
Children anaphylaxis: 0.15 mg IM. Repeat every 5 minutes as needed.
Children severe asthma: 0.01 mg/kg IM up to 0.3 mg. Repeat every 15 minutes.

---

## Key Medications — Salbutamol (Albuterol)

Indication: severe wheezing, asthma, bronchospasm.
Adults: prime inhaler with 5 puffs, give 2 puffs via spacer every 2 minutes until improved.
Children: prime with 5 puffs, give 2 puffs into spacer, 3–5 breaths. Repeat until 6 puffs for under 5 years, or 12 puffs for over 5 years. Can repeat several times per hour for severe wheezing.

---

## Key Medications — Naloxone

Indication: opioid overdose — decreased consciousness, pinpoint pupils, low respiratory rate.
IV: 100 micrograms single dose. IM: 400 micrograms single dose. May repeat every 5 minutes as needed. Long-acting opioids may require 0.4 mg per hour infusion for several hours.

---

## Key Medications — Tranexamic Acid

Indication: severe bleeding following trauma, postpartum haemorrhage.
Trauma: 1 g in 200 mL IV over 10 minutes. Must be given within 3 hours of injury. Follow with continuous infusion of 1 g over 8 hours.
PPH: 1 g IV as soon as possible after birth. Repeat after 30 minutes if bleeding continues.

---

## Key Medications — Paracetamol

Indication: mild to moderate pain, fever, headache.
Adults: 500 mg–1 g orally or rectally every 6 hours. Maximum 4 g daily. Maximum 2 g daily if liver impairment or cirrhosis.
Children: 10–15 mg/kg orally or rectally up to four times per day.

---

## Key Medications — Diazepam (Seizures)

Basic provider rectal route: Adults first dose 10 mg PR. Repeat after 10 minutes if seizure continues.
Children: first dose 0.5 mg/kg PR. Repeat half the first dose after 10 minutes if needed.
Advanced provider IV route: Adults 10 mg slow IV. Children 0.2 mg/kg slow IV. Repeat after 5 minutes if seizure continues. Monitor breathing closely in all patients given diazepam.

---

## Key Medications — Magnesium Sulfate

Indication: eclampsia, seizure in pregnancy or postpartum.
Basic provider: 10 g IM (5 g in each buttock).
Advanced provider: 4 g IV slowly over 20 minutes AND 10 g IM simultaneously. If seizure recurs after 15 minutes: additional 2 g IV over 5 minutes. Transport delays: 5 g IM every 4 hours.

---

## Danger Signs in Children

Airway obstruction: unable to swallow saliva, drooling, stridor.
Breathing: fast breathing, nasal flaring, grunting, chest indrawing.
Circulation: cyanosis (blue lips or fingertips), cold or pale extremities, slow capillary refill.
Disability: altered mental status, lethargy, unusual sleepiness, seizures.
Other: not feeding well, cannot drink, vomiting everything, low body temperature.

---

## Children — Special Assessment Considerations

Children have larger heads and tongues and shorter necks than adults. Position airway correctly for age. Always consider foreign bodies as a cause of breathing problems.
Hypoglycaemia is common in ill children — always check glucose. Children may not report trauma and can have serious internal injury with few external signs. Always check AVPU.

---

## Behavioural Emergencies

Always consider medical or traumatic cause first. Complete full CABCDE assessment. Check glucose.
If agitated or violent: try to calm the patient. Ensure your own safety. If danger to self or others: restrain with soft restraints per local law, may require 5 people. Any restrained patient must be continuously monitored. Monitor airway closely.

---

## Post-Incident Care — Documentation and Handover

Place patient in position of comfort. Transport to closest most appropriate facility. Reassess frequently during transport. Keep patient covered.
Be alert for signs of abuse or human trafficking — contact appropriate authorities per local protocol.
Document the patient encounter. Hand over to receiving facility with full report of findings and treatment given.
