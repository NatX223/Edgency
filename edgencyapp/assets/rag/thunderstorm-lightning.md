## Thunderstorm — What It Is

A thunderstorm is a dangerous storm that always includes lightning. Thunderstorms include powerful winds above 50 miles per hour, create lightning and hail, and can cause flash flooding and tornadoes. Lightning is a leading cause of injury and death from weather-related hazards. Thunderstorms can occur year-round and at any hour.

---

## Thunderstorm — Immediate Action: Go Indoors

When thunder roars, go indoors immediately. A sturdy building is the safest place to be during a thunderstorm. Move from outdoors into a building or a car. If you can hear thunder, you are close enough to be struck by lightning. Do not wait for rain to start before seeking shelter. Do not stand under trees.

---

## Thunderstorm — Safe Shelter: What Counts

A safe shelter is a sturdy, fully enclosed building with plumbing and wiring — not a tent, an open structure, or a carport. A car with a metal top and sides is acceptable if no building is available. Do not touch anything metal inside the vehicle. Avoid convertibles and open vehicles. Identify safe buildings near where you live, work, and spend time before a storm occurs.

---

## Thunderstorm — Indoors: What to Avoid

If you are indoors during a thunderstorm: avoid running water — do not shower, bathe, or wash dishes. Avoid using landline phones — electricity can travel through plumbing and phone lines. Unplug appliances and electronic devices to protect them from power surges. Stay away from windows, doors, and concrete walls or floors.

---

## Thunderstorm — If Outdoors and Cannot Reach Shelter

If you are caught outdoors and cannot reach a building or car: avoid tall isolated trees, hilltops, open fields, and bodies of water. Avoid metal objects such as fences and poles. If in a group, spread out — do not huddle together. Crouch low on the balls of your feet, minimize contact with the ground, and cover your ears. Do not lie flat on the ground.

---

## Thunderstorm — If Boating or Swimming

If boating or swimming when a thunderstorm approaches, get to land and find a sturdy, grounded shelter or vehicle immediately. Water is an excellent conductor of electricity. Lightning can strike the water and electrocute people in or near it over a wide area. Do not wait until the storm is directly overhead — lightning can strike miles from the storm centre.

---

## Thunderstorm — Flash Flooding Risk

Thunderstorms cause flash flooding. Avoid flooded roadways — Turn Around Don't Drown. Just 6 inches of fast-moving water can knock a person down. One foot of moving water can sweep a vehicle away. Do not drive through flooded roads even if they appear shallow. See the Flood & Tsunami reference for full flooding guidance.

---

## Thunderstorm — After: Power Lines and Trees

After a thunderstorm, watch for fallen power lines and trees. Report them immediately to authorities — do not attempt to move them yourself. Pay attention to weather forecasts to know whether additional storms are expected before going outside. Watch for information from authorities regarding potential flash flooding that may follow the storm.

---

## Thunderstorm — Preparedness

Know your area's thunderstorm risk. Sign up for Emergency Alert System (EAS) and NOAA Weather Radio alerts. Cut down or trim trees near your home that may fall during high winds. Secure outdoor furniture before storms. Consider buying surge protectors, lightning rods, or a lightning protection system. Have a communication plan and emergency kit ready.

---

## Lightning Strike — Immediate Medical Response

Lightning strike victims do not carry an electrical charge — it is safe to touch them immediately. Call emergency services first. Check for responsiveness and pulse. If no pulse, begin CPR immediately per local protocol. Lightning can cause cardiac arrest, respiratory arrest, burns at entry and exit points, and blunt trauma from being thrown. Treat as a trauma patient.

---

## Lightning Strike — Injuries to Assess

After a lightning strike: check for cardiac arrest and begin CPR if needed. Assess for burns at the point of contact and exit points (often the feet). Check for spinal injury if the person was thrown or fell. Assess for altered mental status, hearing loss, vision problems, and confusion — all are common after a strike. Follow the altered mental status protocol if needed.

---

## Lightning Strike — Multiple Victims

If there are multiple victims of a lightning strike, treat those who are unresponsive or in cardiac arrest first — unlike most triage situations, lightning victims who appear dead have the highest survival potential with immediate CPR. Those who are conscious and breathing are lower priority. Begin resuscitation on the pulseless victim immediately.

---

## Hail — Immediate Action

Hail can reach the size of a grapefruit and fall at speeds of 100 miles per hour, causing serious injuries and property damage. If hail begins: seek shelter in a sturdy building immediately. If in a vehicle, pull over and stop — do not continue driving. Stay away from windows. Cover your head and neck with your arms if caught outdoors without shelter.

---

## Hail — Injuries from Hail

Large hail causes blunt trauma injuries including head lacerations, fractures, bruising, and in severe cases skull fractures. If a person is injured by hail: assess for head injury, check for altered mental status, control any bleeding with direct pressure. Follow the injured patient protocol. Any loss of consciousness after a hail strike requires urgent transport to hospital.

---

## Thunderstorm — Cross-Reference: Tornado Risk

Thunderstorms can spawn tornadoes with little warning. Signs of a tornado: dark greenish sky, large hail, a loud roar like a freight train, a visible rotating funnel cloud. If a tornado warning is issued: go to the lowest floor of a sturdy building, an interior room away from windows such as a bathroom or closet, and cover your head and neck. Do not shelter under an overpass. Do not stay in a vehicle or mobile home.

---

## Thunderstorm — Cross-Reference: Flooding After Storm

Flash flooding often follows thunderstorms — the most dangerous combination is a storm that produces both heavy rain and strong winds that block drainage. After a thunderstorm, avoid low-lying areas, drainage channels, and areas near rivers or streams. Water levels can rise within minutes. See the Flood & Tsunami reference for full flooding and water safety guidance.

---

## Storm Emergency — Scene Safety for Responders

Before entering a storm-affected scene: confirm the thunderstorm or lightning activity has passed — do not respond into an active lightning environment. Watch for downed power lines, unstable trees, and flooded roadways. Do not touch any electrical equipment near water. Coordinate with local emergency management. If a tornado has caused structural damage, assess buildings for stability before entry.
