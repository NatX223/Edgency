## Flood — What It Is

A flood is a temporary overflow of water onto land that is normally dry. It is the most common natural disaster in the world. Floods can happen anywhere and can occur slowly over many days or happen very quickly. They can cause power outages, pollute drinking water, and damage homes and infrastructure.

---

## Flood — Causes

Floods result from rainfall, snowmelt, coastal or tropical storms, storm surge, and overflows of dams and other water systems. Flash floods can develop within minutes with no warning. Always monitor local weather reports and emergency alerts when heavy rain is forecast or ongoing.

---

## Flood — Critical Rule: Turn Around, Don't Drown

Do NOT walk, swim, or drive through floodwaters under any circumstances. Just 6 inches of fast-moving water can knock a person down. Just 1 foot of moving water can sweep a vehicle — including an SUV — off the road. Floodwater poses a drowning risk for everyone regardless of their ability to swim.

---

## Flood — If Rising Water: Move Up, Not Out

Move to higher ground or the highest level of a building to stay above rising floodwater. Do NOT become trapped in a basement — exits may be blocked as water rises. Do NOT climb into a closed attic — you could become trapped with no escape if water keeps rising. Go to an upper floor with a window instead.

---

## Flood — Bridges and Roads

Stay off bridges over fast-moving water. Fast-moving water can wash bridges away without warning. Never drive around barriers blocking a flooded road — floodwaters may have damaged the road or undermined it. Do not drive into underground roads or through tunnels during a flood.

---

## Flood — Evacuating

Evacuate immediately when ordered to by local officials. Do not underestimate the flood threat by delaying evacuation when it is safe to do so. Take critical supplies, medications, and important documents with you. Plan to text rather than call — phone systems may be overwhelmed during flood events.

---

## Flood — If You Cannot Evacuate: Shelter in Place

If evacuation is not possible, move to the highest floor of a sturdy building. Stay away from windows. Listen for emergency broadcasts. Signal from a window using a bright cloth or flashlight. Do not attempt to wade through floodwater to reach another building — wait for rescue.

---

## Flood — Electrical Hazards

Do not touch electrical equipment if it is wet or if you are standing in water — risk of electrocution. Be alert for downed or unstable trees, utility poles, and power lines after a flood. Underground power lines can electrically charge standing water. Only use generators and fuel-burning equipment outdoors and at least 20 feet from any building opening to avoid carbon monoxide poisoning.

---

## Flood — After: Water Safety

Do not assume your drinking water is safe after a flood — check with your local health department before consuming any tap water. Stay away from floodwater after the event — it may contain sharp objects, downed power lines, sewage, bacteria, chemicals, diseased insects, and stray animals. Wear protective gear when cleaning up flood debris.

---

## Flood — After: Return and Recovery

Return to your home only after local officials confirm it is safe. Document all property damage with photographs before cleaning anything. Contact your flood insurer to file a claim. Register with FEMA for additional assistance. Hire a qualified flood adjuster before attempting repairs. Standard homeowner's insurance does not cover flooding.

---

## Flood — Preparedness: Know Your Risk

Know your area's flood risk before disaster strikes. Sign up for emergency alerts from your local emergency management office. Develop both an evacuation plan and a shelter-in-place plan — evacuation is not always the safest option. Practice your evacuation route. Know what flood watches and warnings mean in your area.

---

## Flood — Preparedness: Emergency Kit

Gather enough food, water, and emergency supplies to last several days. Include all medications and specific needs for each person. Have extra batteries and chargers for mobile devices. Keep important documents in a waterproof, fireproof container and create password-protected digital copies.

---

## Flood — Preparedness: Protect Property

Before a flood: move valued items to higher levels. Elevate critical utilities. Install a back valve and a battery-operated sump pump. Declutter drains and gutters. Use sandbags to create a flood barrier around your property. Purchase flood insurance — standard policies do not cover flooding.

---

## Tsunami — What It Is

A tsunami is a series of enormous ocean waves caused by earthquakes, underwater landslides, volcanic eruptions, or asteroids. Tsunami waves travel at 20–30 miles per hour with heights of 10–100 feet. They can kill or injure people and destroy buildings and infrastructure as waves repeatedly come in and go out.

---

## Tsunami — Where It Can Happen

Tsunamis can happen anywhere along US coasts. Coasts that border the Pacific Ocean or the Caribbean Sea have the greatest risk. A tsunami causes flooding and creates major problems with transportation, power, communications, and drinking water. Multiple waves may arrive hours apart — the first is not always the largest.

---

## Tsunami — Natural Warning Signs

Learn the natural warning signs of a tsunami: a strong or prolonged earthquake near a coast, a loud roar from the ocean, unusual ocean behaviour such as a sudden rise or wall of water, or sudden draining of water that exposes the ocean floor. Any of these signs means evacuate immediately — do not wait for an official warning.

---

## Tsunami — Immediate Action: Earthquake Comes First

If you are in a tsunami hazard area and an earthquake occurs, protect yourself from the earthquake first. Drop to hands and knees. Cover your head and neck with your arms. Hold on to sturdy furniture until shaking stops. Only after shaking stops do you move to higher ground.

---

## Tsunami — Immediate Action: Get to High Ground

When shaking stops or any natural tsunami sign appears, move immediately to a safe place as high and as far inland as possible. Do not wait for official warnings or evacuation orders — natural signs are sufficient to act. Pick shelter 100 feet or more above sea level or at least one mile inland.

---

## Tsunami — Evacuation Routes

Evacuation routes are often marked with a wave symbol and an arrow pointing in the direction of higher ground. Leave immediately if told to do so by authorities. If you are outside the tsunami hazard zone when a warning is issued, stay where you are unless officials say otherwise.

---

## Tsunami — If in the Water or a Boat

If you are in the water during a tsunami, grab onto something that floats such as a raft, tree trunk, or door. If you are in a boat in open water, face the direction of the waves and head out to sea — tsunami waves are less dangerous in deep water. If you are in a harbour when a warning is issued, go inland immediately.

---

## Tsunami — After: Stay Away Until Cleared

After a tsunami, listen to local alerts and authorities for information on areas to avoid and shelter locations. Stay away from damaged buildings, roads, and bridges. Avoid wading in floodwater — it can contain dangerous debris and may be deeper than it appears. Save phone calls for emergencies only. Use text or social media to communicate with family.

---

## Tsunami — Preparedness: Know Your Zone

If you live near or visit a coastal area, learn the tsunami risk for that area. Some communities have evacuation zone maps and marked routes. If you are a visitor, ask the hotel or local officials about community emergency plans. Know your routes from home, work, and recreational areas before a warning is issued.

---

## Tsunami — Preparedness: Family Plan

Create a family emergency communication plan with an out-of-state contact. Plan meeting points in case family members are separated. Sign up for the Emergency Alert System and NOAA Weather Radio. Practice your evacuation route. Know where the marked shelters are in your area.

---

## Flood and Tsunami — Drowning Risk in Floodwater

Both floods and tsunamis create moving water that can drown people quickly. Never enter floodwater on foot — 6 inches can knock you down. Never drive through floodwater — 1 foot can sweep a vehicle away. In tsunami water, grab any floating object. In both situations, moving water is far more dangerous than still water of the same depth.

---

## Flood and Tsunami — Earthquake Connection

Earthquakes beneath the ocean commonly trigger tsunamis. Coastal earthquakes can also trigger flood-related hazards including storm surge and landslides that dam rivers. If you are near a coast and feel an earthquake: Drop, Cover, Hold On during shaking, then move immediately inland and to high ground without waiting for an official warning.

---

## Flood and Tsunami — Electrical and Contamination Hazards

Both floods and tsunamis leave standing water that is hazardous. Downed power lines can electrically charge standing water — never walk through water near power lines. Floodwater may contain sewage, chemicals, bacteria, sharp debris, and wild animals. Do not consume tap water after either event without official confirmation it is safe.

---

## Flood and Tsunami — Medical Emergencies in Water

Drowning survivors may appear conscious but deteriorate rapidly — always monitor airway and breathing. If a drowning survivor has no pulse, begin CPR immediately and continue until help arrives. Handle drowning survivors gently — hypothermia is common. Keep them warm, monitor airway closely, and transport urgently to hospital. Check for other trauma injuries from debris impact.

---

## Flood and Tsunami — Injuries from Debris

Flooding and tsunami waves carry debris at high speed, causing lacerations, fractures, crush injuries, and blunt head trauma. Assess injured patients using the primary survey: control bleeding first, check airway, assess breathing, evaluate circulation. Immobilize spine if head or neck trauma is suspected. Do not move patients through floodwater to reach them unless the scene is safe.

---

## Flood and Tsunami — Scene Safety for Responders

Before entering any flood or tsunami-affected area: assess for ongoing hazards including moving water, electrified water near power lines, structural instability, gas leaks, contaminated water, and secondary wave risk for tsunamis. Do not enter moving water without a flotation device and tethered rescue equipment. Do not touch any electrical equipment near water. Coordinate with local emergency management before committing resources.
