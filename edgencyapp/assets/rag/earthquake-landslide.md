---

## Earthquake — What It Is

An earthquake is the sudden, rapid shaking of the earth caused by the breaking and shifting of underground rock. Earthquakes give no warning. They can happen anywhere but higher-risk areas include California, Alaska, and the Mississippi Valley.

---

## Earthquake — Secondary Hazards

Earthquakes can collapse buildings and cause heavy items to fall, resulting in injuries and property damage. They can also trigger fires, damage roads, cause tsunamis, trigger landslides, and cause avalanches. Always be alert for secondary hazards after shaking stops.

---

## Earthquake — Immediate Action: Drop, Cover, Hold On

If an earthquake happens, protect yourself immediately. DROP to your hands and knees. COVER your head and neck with your arms. Crawl only as far as needed to reach cover from falling materials. HOLD ON to any sturdy furniture until the shaking stops.

---

## Earthquake — If Indoors During Shaking

If indoors, stay there until the shaking stops. Do NOT run outside during shaking — falling debris at exits is a major cause of injury. Do not stand in a doorway. If in a high-rise building, expect fire alarms and sprinklers to activate. Do not use elevators.

---

## Earthquake — If in Bed During Shaking

If you are in bed when an earthquake starts, stay there. Cover your head and neck with a pillow. Do not try to move to another location. Getting out of bed during shaking risks injury from falling objects and broken glass on the floor.

---

## Earthquake — If Outdoors During Shaking

If outdoors when an earthquake starts, stay outdoors. Move away from buildings, streetlights, and utility wires. Once in the open, drop to your knees and stay there until the shaking stops. The greatest danger outdoors is near building exteriors where falling glass and debris land.

---

## Earthquake — If in a Vehicle During Shaking

If you are in a vehicle, pull over and stop. Choose a clear area away from buildings, trees, overpasses, underpasses, and utility wires. Stay inside the vehicle until the shaking stops. If a power line falls on the vehicle, stay inside and wait for help.

---

## Earthquake — If Near Slopes or Cliffs

If you are near slopes, cliffs, or mountains during an earthquake, be alert for falling rocks and landslides immediately. Move away from cliff edges and slope bases. Earthquakes are a leading trigger of landslides — do not assume the danger ends when shaking stops.

---

## Earthquake — After: Check Yourself and Your Building

After shaking stops, check yourself for injury before helping others. If you are in a damaged building, go outside immediately and move quickly away from the building. Do not re-enter damaged buildings. Wear sturdy shoes and work gloves when moving through debris.

---

## Earthquake — After: If Trapped

If you are trapped after an earthquake, send a text message first — phone calls may overwhelm networks. Bang on a pipe or wall to signal your location. Use a whistle if available. Cover your mouth with clothing for dust protection. Shouting uses energy and stirs up dust — use it only as a last resort.

---

## Earthquake — After: Tsunami Risk

If you are in a coastal area or a low-lying area that may experience tsunamis, go inland or to higher ground immediately after the shaking stops. Do not wait for an official warning. The time between an earthquake and a tsunami can be very short — move immediately.

---

## Earthquake — After: Aftershocks

Expect aftershocks to follow the largest shock. Aftershocks can cause additional structural damage and trigger secondary hazards such as landslides and fires. Each time you feel an aftershock, Drop, Cover, and Hold On again. Stay out of damaged buildings during aftershock sequences.

---

## Earthquake — After: Communication

Save phone calls for life-threatening emergencies only. Phone systems are often overwhelmed or down after a major earthquake. Use text messages or social media to communicate with family and friends. Have a pre-arranged out-of-state contact that all family members can reach.

---

## Earthquake — Preparedness: Secure Your Home

Before an earthquake: secure televisions, wall-hanging objects, and heavy furniture. Store heavy and breakable objects on low shelves. Consider retrofitting your building if it has structural vulnerabilities. Standard homeowner's insurance does not cover earthquake damage — consider a separate earthquake insurance policy.

---

## Earthquake — Preparedness: Emergency Kit

Build a supply kit that includes several days of food and water, a flashlight, fire extinguisher, whistle, and a first aid kit. Include each person's specific medications and medical needs. Do not forget pet needs. Keep extra batteries and charging devices for phones and critical equipment.

---

## Earthquake — Preparedness: Family Plan

Create a family emergency communication plan with an out-of-state contact. Plan meeting locations in case family members are separated. Practice Drop, Cover, and Hold On regularly with all family members and coworkers so the response is automatic when an earthquake strikes.

---

## Landslide — What It Is

A landslide is rocks, earth, or other materials moving down a slope. A mudflow is a type of landslide combined with up to 60 percent water. Landslides can travel at 55–100 miles per hour and strike with little or no warning. They cause 25–50 deaths and over a billion dollars in damage each year in the United States.

---

## Landslide — Causes and Triggers

Landslides are caused by rain, earthquakes, volcanoes, or changes to the land such as construction and deforestation. They can also result from flooding. Because earthquakes are a leading trigger of landslides, always watch for landslide risk immediately after an earthquake, especially near slopes.

---

## Landslide — Warning Signs: Ground and Structures

Watch for these signs of an approaching or developing landslide: cracks or bulges in the ground, pavement, or sidewalks; soil moving away from foundations; tilting patios or foundations; broken water lines; leaning telephone poles, trees, walls, or fences. Any of these signs requires immediate evacuation.

---

## Landslide — Warning Signs: Sounds and Water

Additional warning signs of a landslide: unusual sounds such as cracking trees, boulders knocking together, or rumbling that increases in volume. A sudden increase or decrease in water levels in a stream. Water that turns brown or muddy with debris. These signs mean a landslide may be seconds away — move immediately.

---

## Landslide — Immediate Action: Evacuation

If you are under a landslide warning, find safe shelter right away. Evacuate early — do not wait for the landslide to start. It is far safer to evacuate unnecessarily than to be caught in a slide. Evacuate especially if a landslide could occur at night when visibility is poor and reaction time is reduced.

---

## Landslide — Immediate Action: During a Landslide

If a landslide is approaching and you cannot evacuate: move to the upper floors of a sturdy building. If outside, run to the nearest high ground out of the path of the slide. If escape is impossible, curl into a tight ball and protect your head with your arms. Avoid river valleys and low-lying areas.

---

## Landslide — If in a Vehicle

If you are in a vehicle and a landslide is approaching: do not try to outrun it on the road. Leave the vehicle immediately and move uphill and away from the path of the slide. Vehicles can be swept away by even small mudflows. Never park vehicles near steep slopes, cliff bases, or drainage channels.

---

## Landslide — Watch for Flooding After

Floods often follow landslides because they are triggered by the same event — heavy rain or an earthquake. After a landslide, watch for rising water in valleys and low-lying areas. Stay away from drainage channels, stream beds, and river banks. Do not assume a landslide has ended the danger.

---

## Landslide — After: Stay Away and Report

After a landslide, listen to authorities before returning to any affected area. Stay away from downed power lines and report them immediately to emergency services. Check for injuries. Save phone calls for emergencies. Use text messages or social media to communicate with family.

---

## Landslide — After: Building Safety

Do not enter buildings that were in or near the path of a landslide without professional inspection. Check foundations, chimney, and surrounding land for damage. Look for broken utility lines. Watch for secondary slides. Report damage to local emergency management.

---

## Landslide — Preparedness: Know Your Risk

Know the landslide risk in your area before a disaster strikes. Contact local officials or emergency management to ask for landslide hazard maps. Learn the types and signs of landslides common in your region. Sign up for your community's warning system, the Emergency Alert System (EAS), and NOAA Weather Radio for alerts.

---

## Landslide — Preparedness: Evacuation Plan

Know your community's landslide evacuation plans in advance. Practice driving evacuation routes and identify shelter locations before a warning is issued. Gather emergency supplies in case you must leave immediately or if services are cut off. Include medications, food, water, and pet needs.

---

## Landslide — Preparedness: Property

Avoid building in landslide-risk areas such as steep slopes, properties near cliffs, or near drainage ways and streams. Plant ground cover on slopes to reduce soil movement. Build retaining walls to redirect debris flow around buildings. Review your insurance — landslide damage is typically not covered by standard policies.

---

## Earthquake Triggering a Landslide — Combined Response

Earthquakes frequently trigger landslides, particularly near slopes, cliffs, hillsides, and mountains. If an earthquake occurs while you are in or near a hilly or mountainous area: follow Drop, Cover, Hold On during shaking, then immediately after shaking stops, move away from slopes and elevated areas. Do not assume the shaking ending means you are safe — assess your surroundings for landslide risk before moving.

---

## Earthquake + Landslide — If Trapped by Debris

If trapped by earthquake or landslide debris: text for help first. Bang on pipes or walls to signal rescuers. Use a whistle. Cover your mouth to filter dust. Stay as calm as possible to conserve oxygen and energy. Do not light a match or use an open flame — gas lines may be ruptured nearby.

---

## Earthquake + Landslide — Medical Emergencies in Debris

Crush injuries are common in both earthquake and landslide events. Patients trapped for more than one hour risk crush syndrome (rhabdomyolysis): dark urine, severe muscle pain, swelling, risk of kidney failure. Start IV fluids before and during extrication if possible. Do not apply a tourniquet to a crush injury unless there is life-threatening uncontrolled bleeding. Transport urgently to hospital.

---

## Earthquake + Landslide — Assessing Injuries After

After an earthquake or landslide: check for bleeding, fractures, head injuries, and spinal injuries before moving anyone. Immobilize the spine if trauma is suspected. Control active bleeding with direct pressure. For patients with altered mental status after head trauma from falling debris, assess pupils and maintain oxygen saturation above 92%. Transport to the closest appropriate facility urgently.

---

## Earthquake + Landslide — Scene Safety for Responders

Before entering a post-earthquake or post-landslide scene: assess for ongoing hazards — aftershocks, secondary slides, structural instability, gas leaks, downed power lines, fire risk, and flooding. Do not enter an unsafe structure. Coordinate with other responders. Maintain situational awareness at all times. If the scene becomes unsafe, withdraw immediately and reassess.
